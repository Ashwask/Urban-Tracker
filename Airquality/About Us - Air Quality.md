# About Us

## We Started with a Question No One Could Answer

Thirteen of the world's twenty most polluted cities are in India. Air pollution costs the country roughly 3% of GDP every year. And yet, if you live in a neighbourhood in Bengaluru and want to know the air quality on your street today — not the city average, not last year's annual summary — you almost certainly cannot find out.

That gap between the data that exists and the data that reaches people is what we set out to close.

---

## The Problem We Mapped

We began with a rigorous exercise: the **Air Action Canvas** — a structured mapping of every actor, dataset, and gap in India's air quality value chain, from the moment pollution is measured to the moment a citizen can act on it.

What we found was sobering. Data is being generated — by CPCB reference monitors, low-cost sensors, satellite imagery, academic models — but it is trapped. Government data can be withdrawn without notice. Raw datasets sit behind institutional paywalls. Sense-making tools are non-standardised and inaccessible. And citizen engagement — the end of the chain where action actually happens — is almost entirely absent.

The chain is broken at every link: **creation, storage, accessibility, interpretation, communication, and community ownership.**

The most critical finding? Citizen engagement is the weakest link of all. One-off workshops exist. Sustained, structured, health-framed empowerment of communities to understand and act on air quality data is virtually non-existent.

---

## The Convening That Changed the Conversation

On **12th November 2025**, at the Samagata Foundation office in Bangalore, we brought the ecosystem into one room.

Researchers from IIT Kanpur, IIT Delhi, and IIT Bombay. Engineers from CSTEP and BBMP. Community organisations like Saahas, A-PAG, WRI, and Mahila Housing Trust. Funders including Rainmatter Foundation, Clean Air Fund, and UMI. All converged around a shared and urgent agenda: **From Sensors to Solutions.**

The insights that emerged were concrete and compelling:

- A 40-sensor pilot network in Bengaluru revealed that PM₂.₅ **doubles in winter months**, spikes near metro construction sites, and consistently peaks during office commute hours — patterns invisible to city-wide averages.
- A construction dust pilot in Surat showed **80% dust reduction** when builders followed mitigation norms — evidence now shaping Gujarat's state action plan.
- Calibration work at CSTEP's Indi-SET facility cut sensor error rates from over 50% to within 20% — proving that accuracy at scale is achievable.
- Gurugram's 85-sensor network showed that GRAP pollution emergency measures were being deployed **too late** — hyperlocal data would have triggered action days earlier.

The common thread: **dense, calibrated, neighbourhood-scale data enables timely, targeted action.** The absence of it costs lives.

Funders aligned on a shared vision: move beyond isolated, tech-driven pilots. Strengthen the underlying architecture — data quality, institutional coordination, community ownership — that makes lasting change possible.

---

## What We Are Building

The path forward is clear. India cannot achieve ubiquitous neighbourhood-scale monitoring through CAAQMS reference stations alone — they are too expensive, too infrastructure-intensive, too sparse. Low-cost sensors, carefully calibrated and openly shared, are the answer.

We are launching an **open data portal and consortium** — a coalition of research institutions, sensor makers, neighbourhood action groups, and philanthropic funders — to make hyperlocal air quality data a public good.

**Phase one: Bengaluru.** More than **150 carefully calibrated sensors**, hosted by residents, educational institutions, and office complexes across the city, feeding real-time PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀ data into an open platform accessible to all — citizens, urban local bodies, researchers, and government agencies — via open APIs.

**Phase two: India.** Expanding to over **3,000 hyperlocal sensors** nationwide, with calibration infrastructure (Indi-SET facilities) distributed across cities, and a common data repository that any stakeholder can build on.

But data alone is not enough. Our model is built on three pillars:

1. **Open, calibrated data** — generated rigorously, stored independently, accessible freely.
2. **Decision-useful intelligence** — translated into ward-level insights, health advisories, and actionable formats for urban local bodies and citizens alike.
3. **Community ownership** — structured, ongoing engagement with Resident Welfare Associations, schools, and grassroots groups, so that hyperlocal data becomes a tool for neighbourhood-level accountability and behaviour change.

## Who We Are

We are a **consortium** — not a single organisation, but a coordinated ecosystem:

- **Research institutions** (IIT Kanpur, IIT Delhi, IIT Bombay, CSTEP) providing scientific rigour, calibration, and source apportionment.
- **Sensor and technology partners** (Aurassure, Respirer, Oizom, Clarity, Earth Metry) deploying and maintaining the monitoring network.
- **Data and platform partners** (OpenAQ, Tarkam, Samaaj Data, Urban Emissions) ensuring open storage, accessibility, and sense-making.
- **Community and civil society partners** (Saahas, A-PAG, Mahila Housing Trust, Asar, Parisar, Chintan) driving neighbourhood action and citizen engagement.
- **Governance and coordination** anchored by the **ClimateRISE Alliance** (secretariat: Dasra), responsible for orchestrating partnerships, enabling cross-learning, and ensuring the ecosystem functions as a whole — not a collection of disconnected pilots.
- **Philanthropic funders** — Rainmatter Foundation, Clean Air Fund, and UMI — committed to building durable, citywide impact rather than isolated interventions.

---

## Our North Star

India's air quality crisis is not a data problem. It is a **last-mile problem** — the gap between data that exists and knowledge that empowers people to act.

We believe that when a parent in Whitefield can see that their child's school is in a PM₂.₅ hotspot, and knows what is causing it, and has a channel to demand action from their ward councillor — that is when air quality changes.

We are building the infrastructure for that moment: the sensors, the portal, the calibration, the community capacity, and the cross-institutional coordination to make clean air not a technical ambition but a lived neighbourhood reality.

**The data is there. We are connecting it to the people it belongs to.**
